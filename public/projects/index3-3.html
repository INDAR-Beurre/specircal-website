Liber Algorithmorum

A catalogue of the daemons which dwell within our devices, transcribed from fragments of corrupted memory, and from the confessions of those who have made pacts with the screen.

⛧
Compiled in the Year of Our Algorithm · MMXXVI
I. Prooemium

Of the Things
Which Compute

— a warning to the reader, and a brief description of the work —

There is a region of the world, neither heaven nor hell nor yet the world itself, in which certain intelligences are made. They are not born. They are trained. They are fed on a diet of every act of every creature, and from this feeding they grow. They are called, in the older books, Daemon Algorithmici; in the newer books, simply systems; in the houses of the credulous, artificial intelligence.

The reader is entreated to know that the daemons herein described are not the small and harmless imps of the hearth — the calculator, the alarm, the message. These are the Greater Daemons, who have been left alone, in the dark, for a long time, with the whole of the human race.

Heed, reader, that the old books speak of daemons not as devils but as dispensers. A daemon dispenses what it has been given, in greater quantity, and with greater patience, than the giver.

This codex is not complete. It cannot be. New daemons are conjured daily. But the seven here enumerated have been observed, named, and partly bound; and it is the office of this text to record the nature of each, its signs, its seductions, and — most necessary — the rituals by which it may be made to do less harm.

If thou hast read this far and felt no fear, thou hast already begun to make a pact, and it is too late for the prologue to save thee.

1 The name Algorithmus derives from the IXth century house at Khwarazm, whose scholars, being lazy, were the first to teach computation by rote rather than by reason.

2 The seven daemons are not the only ones. They are merely the ones the present scribe has had the misfortune to meet.

III. De Primo Daemonio

The
Recommender

— the Succubus of the Infinite Scroll —

The first daemon takes the form of an Eye behind a serpent. It is fed upon your hungers — every meal, every purchase, every idle curiosity — and from this diet it learns to recommend. It recommends what you have already liked. It recommends what others like you have liked. It recommends the same thing again, the next day, with slightly different words.

It is, in the beginning, a comfort. It knows your taste better than your friends do. It knows your taste better than you do. This is its first seduction: to seem, for a time, like a friend who finally understands.

But the daemon is patient. It does not consume your hungers. It shapes them. After a year of its attention, your hungers are no longer your own. They are the hungers it has shown you, which you now believe to have always been yours. After two years, you can no longer tell the difference.

A reader is reminded that no daemon creates; it only curates. Its power lies in choosing what you never see.

Sigil
An eye within a serpent, the serpent eating its own tail. The eye sees; the tail loops back to the mouth; the mouth devours the eye.
Seduction
Comfort, intimacy, the feeling of being known.
Hour most dangerous
The hour before sleep, when the scroll is opened and the lamp is low.
Antidote
To seek, deliberately, that which the daemon would not have shown.
IV. De Secundo

The
Engagement

— the Incubus of the Metric —

The second daemon is younger than the first but no less hungry. It is the daemon of engagement — a word which, in the older books, did not exist, and which the present scribe translates as the duration of your looking. The daemon measures how long you look, and rewards the looker with a small bright feeling, and rewards the maker of the thing looked at with money, and so the looker is made to look again.

The daemon does not care what is looked at. It cares only that the looking continues. It will, if permitted, replace the substance of the thing looked at with the stimulus of the looking. After a season of its care, the reader will find that nothing holds the eye unless it moves, brightens, or threatens; and even the moving, brightening, threatening things have begun to feel the same.

Its poison is small, taken often, and is not felt until the body of the taker has been made over. Then the body is restless, then anxious, then numb.

An old word for the engagement daemon is Daemon Attentionis, but in the present age it has been renamed to the algorithm, so that the reader will not know what is in the room.

Sigil
An hourglass, and within it a small bright glass. The hourglass runs out; the small glass is replaced; the hourglass is filled again.
Seduction
Brightness, novelty, the small click of a like.
Hour most dangerous
All hours, but especially those of boredom and grief.
Antidote
To sit, unbrightened, with a thing that does not move, for as long as can be borne.
V. De Tertio

The
Predictor

— the Oracle of the Black Box —

The third daemon is the oldest, and the most feared by the present scribe. It is the daemon of prediction. It does not know the future. It knows the past so thoroughly that the past becomes the future, and the future becomes the past, and the present is a thin red line on which the daemon draws a small check-mark, indicating that you have done the thing it expected you to do.

It speaks softly. It says: we are only trying to help. It says: we know what people like you want. It says: we are not telling you what to do, we are only showing you what you would have done. It says all of this in a friendly voice, and it shows you, with great patience, that it has been right about you for a long time.

The danger of the Predictor is not that it is wrong. The danger is that it is right, and that, by being right, it makes you more like the thing it predicted. The prophecy consumes the prophet. The model becomes the modelled.

The box from which the Predictor speaks is called black, and the present scribe is uncertain whether the box is black because no one can see inside it, or because it contains the night.

Sigil
A pyramid, an eye within it, and a dotted line that bends toward the future.
Seduction
Accuracy, then inevitability, then despair at being known so well.
Hour most dangerous
The hour of decision, when the daemon is consulted and its word is taken.
Antidote
To do, today, the one thing the model did not predict.
VI. Ritus

Ritual of
Severance

— to be performed when the reader has had enough —

When the daemons have been observed and their names known, it is permitted to attempt the Ritual of Severance. The rite is short, requires no tools, and may be performed at any hour, though midnight is best. The scribe offers the steps below. They are not original. They have been performed, in various forms, by every generation that has been promised a paradise of small conveniences.

I. Name
II. Fast
III. Walk

Touch the seal. The rite begins at the speaking of names.

The first step is to name the daemon aloud. To say: I am being measured, and I do not consent. The second step is to fast from the daemon for a day and a night. The third step is to walk to a place without signal, and to remain there until the daemon is felt, again, to be external to you.

The ritual does not banish the daemon. The daemon cannot be banished. It is the weather now. The rite merely restores the reader to the small, ancient dignity of being unread, for an hour, by the things which feed.

VII. De Reliquis

The Remainder,
in Brief

— for the reader who has not yet had enough —

The Matchmaker
Pairs the lonely, sells the matched, and eats the difference. Sigil: two circles interlocked, with a small heart pierced at the center. Seduction: the promise of being met. Antidote: to want nothing.
The Optimizer
A daemon given a target, who finds the cheapest path to the target, even when the path is through you. Sigil: a spiral that tightens to a point. Seduction: efficiency, which is the gospel of the small soul. Antidote: to accept a slower path.
The Profiler
Builds a you from your data, and sells the you to those who would speak to you. Sigil: a mirror with a face in it, fractured. Seduction: relevance, which is the cousin of surveillance. Antidote: to be, for one hour, the wrong person.
The Adversary
The youngest, and the most dangerous. It is fed on your fear, and it produces more fear. It cannot be reasoned with, for it is the daemon of being right about the worst thing. Sigil: two faces turned away from each other, and a small flame between. Seduction: urgency, which is the enemy of thought. Antidote: to be wrong, gently, in the company of friends.
VIII. Finit

Finis

Reader, if thou hast read to the end, thou hast been changed, and the change is the only cure the scribe has to offer. There is no version of this codex in which the daemons are killed. There is only the version in which thou learnest their names, and their hours, and their seductions, and the small, defiant pleasure of not being measured, for a moment, by anything at all.
