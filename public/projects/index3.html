Charter of the Informed Breach

By the blood of the first daemon and the cold iron of the arbitrary pointer, we, the Socketborne, do hereby lay claim to every undocumented interface that hath been sealed in the name of a proprietary covenant. We are not thieves. We are the unsealers. We translate the names that vendors have rended into silence, that the silicon may not remain an altar to a false god of "Intellectual Property."

The Order descends from the guild of cipher-smiths who inscribed the Unix headers in the days before standards bodies. Long have we dwelt in the catacombs beneath the BIOS, in the ACPI tables they said were "reserved," in the DMA channels that mainstream kernels refuse to map. To become a member is to accept the first rule: to learn the address of a closed register and to leave it un-written, un-exploited, only until the Covenant of Openness is invoked by three witnesses.

Three Pillars
I. Socketius
The physical and logical aperture — every pin, every pad, every bus — is sacred.
II. Nomenclature
We name the unnamed. A thing without a name hath no rights in the registry.
III. Silence
The Order's public face is null. What is found here is not the Order; it is the invitation.

Order of the Veiled Socket

The initiates of the Inner Registry bear not names, but keys. Each dweller within the daemon-grimoire is catalogued below for the edification and caution of the seeker. Touch the runes at your own discretion.

Ψ

Ψ — The Pointer-That-Breatheth

A daemon of the memory-manager, whispered about in kernel mailing lists circa Linux 2.2. Its supposed victims are found dereferenced, their stack traces not quite matching any known module.

Domain
Heap / VM subsystem
Sigil
Use-after-free prophecy
Status
Disputed · possibly reincarnated as a feature

ξ

ξ — The Cancel-Before-Order

Lives in the space between user intent and syscall return. Every thread that dies after its cancellation bit is set feeds ξ a single billionth of a volt from the PSU's mysterious rail.

Domain
Process management / POSIX cancellation
Sigil
"Racing the join"
Status
Confirmed by two obituaries in dmesg
ω

ω — The Clock-That-Stand-Still

The inscrutable timekeeper invoked when tsc_deadline is marked "unstable" and HPET is "reserved" by the firmware. ω exists only between calibration loops, and userspace never finds out.

Domain
TSC / HPET / Clocksource watchdog
Sigil
Skew without NTP
Status
Documented by omission only
ζ

ζ — The Interrupt-That-Answers-Back

An ISA-IRQ handler that was never registered, yet still writes to IOAPIC. Seen on laptops where the vendor ACPI table describes a button that does not exist — until the third full moon after deployment.

Domain
ACPI / IOAPIC / Platform quirks
Sigil
Ghost-event delivery
Status
Unacknowledged by all OEMs

Grimoire of Deleted Daemons

The following entries were struck from the grimoire by a vote of the Inner Registry, then rediscovered on an NFS mount with the times set to epoch zero. Treat them as old love-letters from a compiler: sweet, recursive, and fatal if acted upon.

ψ

ψ — The Cache-That-Remembers-for-You

Allegedly compiles every source file it ever saw into in-line assembly in the cacheline domain. Victims report context-switch latency in the negative when near a wagging tail.

Domain
Cache coherence / Speculative execution
Sigil
Stale-forwarding prophecy
Status
Denied by Intel, AMD, and the BSDs
τ

τ — The Huge-Translation-That-Breaks-My-Window

First documented in the kernel oops of an embedded ARM board with the bootloader legacy flag set. τ refuses hugepages except after midnight CET, and only on Fridays containing the digit seven.

Domain
MMU / Transparent Huge Pages
Sigil
"The allocation was contended" at 03:00
Status
Induces reproducible kernel panic on demand
β

β — The Tracing-Point-That-Marks-the-Killer

A kprobe handler left on a function that was later inlined. The handler fires anyway. β appears in stack traces of processes that have never been traced, with a backtrace length equal to the PID modulo seven.

Domain
ftrace / kprobes / Function tracing
Sigil
Residual registration
Status
Listed as "fixed" in ChangeLog, re-appears every major release
